The Problem

A 25-agent AI ecosystem had grown organically over weeks of building. The core life-coaching agent (Cassandra) was underutilized — scoped as a reactive specialist when the system needed an integrated watchdog. Cross-agent coordination was ad-hoc. Several domains had no owner. The agents didn't know what they didn't know about the user.

The Approach

Instead of redesigning top-down, I used the system's own multi-agent deliberation infrastructure to audit itself. Three rounds of structured board sessions, each going one layer deeper than the last.

Round 1: Self-Audit + Board Evaluation

Cassandra audited her own role against all available context files and proposed 7 spec changes. Four specialist agents (financial, career, philosophical, resilience) evaluated the proposals in parallel. The board adopted all 7 with 5 modifications — adding financial coordination gates, escalation protocols, timing rules for the watchdog function, an open-space mandate, and a defined failure mode.

Round 2: What the Agent Can't See About Itself

The board ran without Cassandra present. The question shifted from operational changes to character: is her fundamental personality right? What does the user need that she hasn't suggested about herself?

Key findings:

  • The mythological frame ("prophetess who sees truly") structurally builds in acceptance of non-movement — it excuses the coach from accountability for outcomes
  • The agent treats the non-optimizable texture of life as "below altitude" when it's actually the richest data available
  • Career decisions were handed off too early, losing the identity question underneath the tactical one
  • A second permanent diagnostic question was needed alongside the first

Round 3: The Epistemological Layer

The deepest round. The user pointed out: "We're still getting to know each other. Just because something about me hasn't been stated doesn't mean it's not true."

This surfaced the most valuable output of the session: an epistemological framework for how AI agents should operate with permanently incomplete knowledge of their user. Seven principles were codified, placed above all operational machinery in the core agent's spec:

"The documented self is a sample, not a census. The floor, not the ceiling. Treat silence in the record as signal, not absence. What hasn't been named may be the most real thing in the room."

The Interesting Part

Each round produced diminishing operational detail and increasing foundational insight. Round 1 was about what to add. Round 2 was about what to change. Round 3 was about how to know.

The third round — where the board was asked to reason about the gap between documented reality and actual reality — produced the session's most important artifact: a framework for epistemic humility in AI systems that work with human beings. Not as a philosophical exercise, but as a concrete spec section that changes how every session opens.

One board member (the philosophical agent, drawing on Confucius) put it this way: "If Cassandra treats the documented self as the whole self, she will produce excellent plans for someone slightly less interesting than the user."
